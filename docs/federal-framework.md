# Federal Framework
## National Policy for Affordable Basics

This document sets out the federal responsibilities within the Affordable Basics framework. It is not a standalone manifesto. It assumes the governing principles in the core document and focuses on the policy tools that only the federal government can use at meaningful scale.

The federal role is to lower the national cost floor. That means reducing input costs, improving competition in concentrated markets, funding national-scale infrastructure, and shaping the financing rules that affect every state. Washington cannot directly run every essential system well, but it does control the trade, tax, spending, and interstate market rules that determine whether states are operating in a favorable or hostile cost environment.

At the framework level, the federal task is broad: reduce structural costs, improve resilience, and stop national policy from making essentials more expensive than they need to be. At the policy level, the agenda should be blunt: remove tariffs that inflate essential inputs, build transmission and energy capacity, simplify distorted healthcare payment structures, and back food and industrial logistics that strengthen domestic supply.

## Federal First Moves

The federal agenda should start with the highest-leverage actions:

1. Eliminate or narrow tariffs that raise the cost of essential industrial and agricultural inputs without delivering clear strategic benefit
2. Expand transmission, grid interconnection, and energy financing where federal coordination is indispensable
3. Push healthcare pricing and payment structures toward simpler, more comparable, primary-care-friendly models
4. Reduce federal cost pressure in food production through input policy, logistics support, and better-targeted agricultural programs

If the federal framework cannot name first moves, it is still too abstract.

## Federal Responsibilities

### 1. Raw Materials and Industrial Inputs

This is the clearest federal pillar. The federal government controls tariffs, trade treatment, strategic reserves, and many of the tax rules that shape industrial cost structures.

**Federal priorities:**
- Eliminate tariffs on essential industrial and agricultural inputs where those tariffs raise downstream prices without delivering clear strategic benefit
- Provide federal tax relief for qualifying inputs used in food production, energy generation, water infrastructure, and essential manufacturing
- Maintain strategic reserves only where market concentration or geopolitical risk creates a clear vulnerability
- Use competition policy to monitor concentrated supply chains that can raise costs for essential goods

**Why this belongs at the federal level:**
States cannot run national trade policy. A California-only attempt to lower input costs would be partial and easily overwhelmed by federal tariffs, national shipping costs, or concentrated suppliers operating across state lines.

### 2. National Energy Competitiveness

Federal energy policy should aim at abundance, reliability, and lower system-wide costs. The federal government cannot set every retail rate, but it can influence the cost of generation, interstate transmission, capital formation, and permitting on federal land.

**Federal priorities:**
- Support generation investment across multiple technologies when those investments improve reliability and lower long-run costs
- Accelerate interstate transmission and grid interconnection where national coordination is required
- Preserve and expand financing tools for generation, storage, transmission, and grid modernization
- Align industrial policy with the energy demands of manufacturing, data centers, and compute-intensive sectors
- Reduce unnecessary federal permitting friction while preserving core safety and environmental review

**Why this belongs at the federal level:**
The federal government has tools that shape the national energy supply curve. Without those tools, states are left trying to manage retail affordability inside markets they do not fully control.

### 3. National Food and Agricultural Policy

Federal food policy should reduce avoidable cost pressure in the national food system while leaving room for states to adapt to local geography and farming conditions.

**Federal priorities:**
- Reform federal subsidy structures to reward essential crop production, resilience, soil health, and water efficiency rather than volume alone
- Reduce tariff and trade policies that raise the cost of fertilizer, equipment, or other necessary farm inputs
- Support interstate logistics, cold chain, and food processing capacity where federal coordination improves resilience
- Protect a strategic baseline of domestic production in categories where supply dependence creates real vulnerability

**Why this belongs at the federal level:**
Agricultural finance, trade, insurance, and logistics are national systems. States can influence local production conditions, but they cannot by themselves redesign the cost structure of the national food economy.

### 4. Healthcare Market Rules and Baseline Financing

The federal government cannot design every state delivery network, but it does control major payment systems, insurance frameworks, and the basic legal architecture of healthcare finance.

This document keeps healthcare at the summary level. The detailed architecture is split between `docs/basic-care-framework.md` and `docs/earned-healthcare-continuity-insurance-framework.md`.

**Federal priorities:**
- Establish clear, comparable pricing rules for common services so patients, employers, and states can see real costs
- Preserve or expand federal support for preventive care, maternal care, and primary care capacity
- Use Medicare, Medicaid, and federal purchasing leverage to push for simpler and more transparent payment structures
- Encourage reimbursement models that reward primary and preventive care rather than avoidable downstream complexity

**Why this belongs at the federal level:**
Healthcare financing is too large and too integrated for states to solve alone. States can build benefit and access models, but federal policy still determines much of the payment environment those models operate within.

### 5. National Water and Infrastructure Support

Water is primarily a state and regional issue, especially in the American West. The federal role is narrower but still important.

**Federal priorities:**
- Fund large-scale reclamation, storage, and conveyance projects where federal infrastructure support is necessary
- Coordinate through agencies that already shape western water systems
- Support drought resilience, watershed protection, and climate adaptation where interstate or federal land issues are involved

**Why this is a supporting role:**
The federal government should not pretend it can centrally manage state water systems. Its role is to finance, coordinate, and remove bottlenecks where scale or geography requires it.

## Federal Policy Instruments

The federal toolkit in this framework is limited to a few categories:
- Trade and tariff policy
- Tax policy
- Targeted subsidies and credits
- Infrastructure finance
- Competition policy
- National transparency and reporting requirements
- Payment and reimbursement rules in federally funded systems

This is intentional. The federal framework should shape incentives and lower structural costs, not attempt to micromanage local delivery from Washington.

## Federal Financing and Policy Logic

Federal financing logic should follow the kind of problem being solved.

**First**, when federal policy itself is raising costs, the right move is often to remove the burden rather than create a new subsidy. Tariffs, distorted tax treatment, and unnecessary federal delay should usually be reduced directly.

**Second**, where the problem is national infrastructure or system capacity, the federal government should prefer capital finance, credit support, and long-horizon investment over open-ended operating subsidies. Transmission, grid modernization, large logistics systems, and selected water infrastructure fit this category.

**Third**, where households need baseline support through systems the federal government already helps finance, support should move through transparent public programs rather than hidden market distortions.

**Fourth**, recurring subsidies to productive sectors should be narrower, conditional, and easier to justify than broad permanent supports. They should be tied to resilience, cost reduction, or clear strategic need, not simply to political habit.

The federal government is strongest when it lowers structural costs, finances national-scale systems, and sets clear rules. It is weaker when it tries to mask high-cost structures with indefinite subsidy.

## Federal Commitments Within the Framework

At the federal level, the distinction between baseline guarantees and targeted productive support should stay explicit.

**Baseline-support functions:**
- Finance or co-finance household-facing essentials where national programs already operate at scale, especially preventive care, maternal care, and selected infrastructure support
- Set minimum transparency and fairness rules in markets where households cannot realistically shop or bargain effectively

**Targeted productive-support functions:**
- Lower the cost of essential inputs
- Strengthen transmission, logistics, and industrial capacity
- Support sectors whose failure would raise system-wide costs for households and states

The federal government is usually stronger at changing background cost structures than at running local service delivery. The framework should lean into that.

## Tradeoffs and Constraints

The federal framework has to stay within three constraints.

**First**, not every low price is a durable low price. Eliminating a tariff or subsidy without considering national resilience can create future vulnerability.

**Second**, some concentration is a market structure problem, not a tax problem. Price relief may require competition policy, not only tax relief.

**Third**, federal spending still has to be financed. This framework should be honest that some reforms reduce revenue, some require direct expenditure, and some produce savings only over time.

## Measures of Federal Success

Federal performance should be judged by outcomes that Washington can plausibly influence:
- Lower input costs for essential sectors
- Lower energy and logistics costs at the wholesale or system level
- Improved resilience in domestic food and industrial production
- More transparent healthcare pricing and stronger primary-care financing
- Greater national competitiveness in essential industries

## Relationship to California

The federal framework matters to California because it shapes the cost environment the state inherits. But federal policy is only half the story. California still has to govern water, utility affordability, land use, agricultural operations, and primary-care delivery inside the state. Those responsibilities are addressed separately in the California companion document.
