# Earned Healthcare Continuity Insurance Framework
## Work-Earned Major-Medical Continuity Protection

Earned healthcare continuity insurance is the work-earned major-medical continuity layer inside the Affordable Basics framework.

It exists to protect households against emergency, hospital, specialty, and other major non-basic medical costs when ordinary coverage fails or work becomes unstable, without reproducing the current cliff structure that punishes work.

It is not private insurance and it is not comprehensive public insurance. It is a disciplined continuity backstop: real protection against serious medical risk during coverage disruption, clear limits on what is covered, and a structure durable enough to govern.

This document should be read after the [Basic Care Framework](basic-care-framework.md).

## Introduction

Earned healthcare continuity insurance is the layer that protects households from serious non-basic medical risk when ordinary coverage is disrupted after the universal basic-care baseline ends. It is meant to carry households through bridge periods created by job loss, hours loss, voluntary work transitions, employer-plan disruption, or coverage cliffs, while staying disciplined enough that it remains continuity protection rather than turning into a second universal baseline.

This document should be read with one point kept clear from the start: the California version of the framework does **not** depend on a wholesale rewrite of Medi-Cal eligibility. Medi-Cal remains the primary public insurance structure for Californians who already qualify under federal and retained state rules. Earned healthcare continuity insurance exists to solve the cliff and continuity problems outside that core structure, not to pretend the state can cheaply replace it.

This is a bridge model first. California can build a serious continuity bridge inside current law, while leaving room for broader federal reform later if Medicaid and related national financing rules are eventually rewritten around the same two-layer logic.

> Basic care is universal. Medi-Cal stays primary where it already applies. Earned healthcare continuity insurance protects the household when ordinary coverage fails and neither Medi-Cal nor adequate private coverage fills the gap.

The operating hierarchy should be just as clear:

1. `Basic care` first
2. `Medi-Cal` where eligible
3. adequate private coverage where active
4. the bridge only when ordinary coverage fails

## Core Framework

### Why Earned Healthcare Continuity Insurance Exists

Earned healthcare continuity insurance exists to solve three practical problems:

- people should not lose healthcare security because they work more
- households need protection against emergency, hospital, and specialty costs beyond basics
- continuity protection during coverage loss should be simpler and more legible than the current cliff-based system without forcing a full rewrite of Medi-Cal

It should be understood as **work-earned major-medical continuity protection** for non-basic care. That framing clarifies the division of labor:

- basic care handles routine universal essentials
- Medi-Cal remains the primary public insurance platform for eligible Californians
- earned healthcare continuity insurance handles serious non-basic medical risk during periods when ordinary coverage has broken down and no better primary coverage exists
- private coverage competes by being richer, easier, and lower-friction

In the California version of the framework, the continuity layer should explicitly cover the bridge cases that households actually face:

- involuntary job loss
- hours reductions that end employer coverage
- voluntary work transitions, including entrepreneurship or movement into self-employment
- employer-plan termination or unaffordable continuation after separation
- coverage cliffs, where public coverage ends before replacement coverage is actually usable

### What Earned Healthcare Continuity Insurance Covers

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should cover serious non-basic healthcare costs during periods of coverage disruption, but it should not try to be the richest or most convenient coverage tier in the system.

#### Covered non-basic care

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should cover:

- emergency room costs beyond the universal basic layer
- inpatient hospitalization
- specialist-led treatment
- surgery
- advanced imaging
- complex oncology, cardiology, neurology, and similar care
- complex behavioral health and long-term supports where applicable
- medically necessary established drug therapies under program rules

The main exception is pregnancy and childbirth, which remain fully inside the basic-care package even where care crosses ordinary outpatient boundaries.

#### What earned healthcare continuity insurance is not

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should be real protection, but it should not try to imitate richer private coverage. Private coverage should remain better on:

- lower cost sharing
- broader formularies
- easier specialist access
- lower administrative friction
- broader non-essential or discretionary benefits

That difference gives households a real incentive to move into stronger private coverage without making continuity protection fake or inhumane.

#### Drug coverage rule

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should **not** be generics-only.

That would undermine the purpose of continuity insurance, because many serious non-basic conditions require drugs that have no generic equivalent. A public major-medical continuity layer that refuses medically necessary established non-generic treatment would stop being a real backstop.

The sharper distinction should be this:

- basic care uses a narrow essential outpatient drug layer that is mostly generic-first
- earned healthcare continuity insurance covers medically necessary established drugs for covered non-basic conditions, but through a disciplined public formulary
- private coverage remains more attractive because its formulary is broader, easier to use, and less restrictive

Recommended position:

- use generic-first rules where a generic equivalent exists
- use biosimilar-first rules where appropriate
- require utilization controls for high-cost specialty drugs where clinically defensible
- exclude convenience brands when lower-cost clinically appropriate alternatives exist
- exclude experimental, investigational, and non-established drug use

This creates a cleaner distinction. Private coverage is richer. Earned healthcare continuity insurance is real, but managed and cost-conscious.

#### Coverage boundary for advanced and experimental care

The work-earned continuity major-medical layer should cover serious care that is medically necessary and already part of accepted practice. It should not become an open-ended promise to fund every novel or discretionary intervention.

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should cover **advanced but established** care such as:

- advanced imaging when medically necessary
- specialist surgery
- cancer treatment that is standard of care
- hospital-level treatment for serious illness

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should exclude care that is:

- experimental
- investigational
- not yet established as standard treatment for the condition at issue
- primarily elective or convenience-enhancing rather than medically necessary
- luxury in service setting rather than medically necessary in clinical content

#### Coverage boundary test

Cover the service when all of the following are true:

- the service is medically necessary
- the service is established or evidence-supported for the condition at issue
- the service fits inside the defined continuity-insurance benefit class
- the service is not primarily a luxury, convenience, or enhancement purchase

Exclude the service when any of the following are true:

- the service is experimental or investigational
- the service is outside accepted treatment standards for the condition at issue
- the service is primarily elective, concierge, luxury, or convenience-oriented
- the service is mainly an enhancement rather than a medical necessity

#### Concise governing rule

> Earned healthcare continuity insurance covers medically necessary established non-basic care during periods of coverage disruption. It does not guarantee experimental, investigational, luxury, or discretionary high-cost care outside accepted standards of treatment.

### How Households Earn and Use Earned Healthcare Continuity Insurance

The framework should reject the idea that working more should make healthcare less secure.

#### Core principle

No one should lose healthcare protection because they worked more hours or earned more wages at the margin. The current cliff structure creates exactly the wrong incentive. Earned healthcare continuity insurance should replace that logic with a simpler earned-protection model tied to work history and coverage continuity.

The bridge should not depend on whether a work change was voluntary or involuntary. If a household has earned banked bridge protection and adequate replacement coverage is not active, the bridge should be available during a qualifying transition. That matters for layoffs, but it also matters for entrepreneurship, self-employment, and other ordinary work changes that the framework should not punish.

#### Hours-bank structure

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should operate through a statewide bank of earned household coverage credits.

Core design features:

- work hours generate coverage credits
- payroll-tax contributions help finance the bridge layer
- credits accumulate proportionally for part-time as well as full-time work
- credits convert into **months of household bridge coverage**
- credits can carry forward up to a defined cap
- credits draw down by time, not by medical episode or claims utilization

The point of the bank is to make bridge coverage portable, legible, and connected to work without punishing uneven work histories, short-term volatility, or simple medical bad luck.

#### Household-month coverage rule

The bank should be redeemed in **household-month units**, not in service-specific draws.

Recommended position:

- one month of earned healthcare continuity insurance protects the eligible household unit for one month
- the household unit should include the covered adult or adults and dependent children under the program's family rules
- a family does not lose bank faster simply because it uses more care in a covered month

This keeps the bridge layer focused on continuity of protection rather than on rationing care episode by episode.

#### Accrual rule

The credit-earning rule should stay simple:

- **each taxed work hour earns 0.008 household-month credits of bridge coverage**
- **credited hours should be capped at 150 hours per month**

That means:

- `50` taxed hours earn `0.4` months of bridge coverage
- `100` taxed hours earn `0.8` months of bridge coverage
- `125` taxed hours earn `1.0` month of bridge coverage
- `150` taxed hours earn the monthly maximum of `1.2` months of bridge coverage
- partial work still earns proportionate protection with no monthly cliff

This is a better bridge rule than the looser earlier version. A worker can still build meaningful protection through steady work, but the bank no longer fills so quickly that the bridge starts to look like a second standing entitlement. The `150`-hour ceiling still rewards strong work months while preventing unusually high-hour periods from turning into unlimited bank-building.

The political and administrative point should stay explicit: this is earned continuity protection, not an unlimited new entitlement. The bank is real, but it is capped, time-limited, and tied to taxed work history.

#### Bank cap

The framework should also cap the total amount a household can accumulate.

Recommended position:

- the household bank should cap at **12.0 months** of earned healthcare continuity protection
- once the household reaches that cap, additional credited hours should not add more months until the balance falls

This keeps the system pro-work while making the bridge visibly temporary. In political and administrative terms, up to one year of earned continuity protection is easier to explain and defend than a longer bank while still giving households real time to navigate layoffs, work transitions, startup risk, and cliff-driven disruptions.

#### High-income draw suspension

The framework may still need an income ceiling for drawing on the bridge layer, but it should not erase earned credits.

Recommended position:

- above a defined income threshold, a household may stop drawing on the bridge bank
- earned credits should not disappear simply because income temporarily rises
- if income later falls, the banked credits should still be available subject to program rules

This creates a draw suspension, not a punishment for work.

#### Children and family coverage

Children should have an advantaged place in the framework, but that advantage should mostly be expressed through universal basics rather than through a separate child-only means-tested layer.

When a child needs care beyond the universal basic-care layer, access to earned healthcare continuity insurance should follow the household unit.

Recommended position:

- dependent children are part of the household unit for purposes of earned healthcare continuity insurance
- one month of household continuity coverage protects the whole eligible household for that month
- rare or higher-acuity childhood needs outside basics should therefore be covered through the household's earned continuity protection
- if a child has a serious non-basic need during a verified transition or administrative gap, the child should not lose access solely because of timing friction in bank activation, redetermination, or claims routing

This is intentionally pro-family. It gives parents a clear incentive to build and maintain household protection through work, while still ensuring that ordinary childhood care is already protected inside universal basics and that serious pediatric need is not left exposed by paperwork or timing failures.

### Handoff and Order of Responsibility

Earned healthcare continuity insurance only works if the handoff with emergency care, private coverage, the marketplace, and Medi-Cal is simple enough for households to understand.

#### Emergency care

Emergency care should be treated separately from basic care.

The framework should not imply that emergency room financing belongs inside the universal basic-care package. Emergency care is too expensive, too variable, and too high-acuity to define the baseline layer.

The framework should distinguish between emergency **access** and emergency **public payment**.

Recommended position:

- no one should be denied emergency screening or stabilization
- pregnancy and labor should remain fully protected
- the earned healthcare continuity insurance bank should determine monthly eligibility for public payment of emergency and higher-acuity costs beyond the universal basic layer

This means the bank can govern public financial responsibility without pretending that people can be turned away from emergency care at the point of crisis.

#### Non-emergent ER use

The framework should also discourage routine abuse of the emergency room without forcing patients to self-diagnose in dangerous ways.

Recommended position:

- emergency departments still screen patients
- if a visit is ultimately classified as non-emergent and within the ordinary scope of basic urgent care, public reimbursement can be limited to an urgent-care-equivalent payment logic rather than a full ER-cost logic

This is a cleaner anti-abuse rule than pretending emergency access itself can be bank-limited.

#### Order of responsibility

The non-basic-care handoff has to be simple or the entire model becomes confusing.

The order should be:

1. `Basic care` always pays for covered basic-care services.
2. `Medi-Cal` remains primary wherever Medi-Cal eligibility and coverage already apply.
3. Adequate employer or private coverage remains primary when it exists.
4. `Earned healthcare continuity insurance` becomes the work-earned continuity major-medical option only when those other major systems do not adequately cover the household.

The framework should prefer clean primary responsibility over overlapping payment chaos. If a reader cannot explain the sequence in one sentence, the design is too complicated.

#### Private insurance adequacy

Private insurance should remain the more attractive and richer option, but it should not displace earned healthcare continuity insurance unless it is genuinely adequate.

Private coverage should only displace earned healthcare continuity insurance if it meets a meaningful adequacy test.

That test should be objective enough to prevent both public crowd-out of real private coverage and private displacement by junk coverage. At minimum, adequate private coverage should meet all of the following:

- a defined actuarial-value floor, such as a bronze-equivalent or stronger major-medical standard
- a defined annual household out-of-pocket ceiling below a public adequacy threshold
- network adequacy rules that provide real access to hospital, emergency, specialty, and essential outpatient care
- access to medically necessary established drugs for covered serious conditions
- no exclusion structure that leaves the household exposed to ordinary catastrophic medical risk while technically "covered"

This matters because nominal coverage on paper is not the same thing as real protection in practice.

Working rule:

- if a household has active bank months and lacks adequate private coverage, earned healthcare continuity insurance becomes the continuity major-medical option
- if a household has adequate employer or private coverage, that coverage remains primary for non-basic care
- banked months should be preserved rather than erased while adequate private coverage is active
- the marketplace remains the main buy-up and transition venue above continuity insurance

#### Anti-bureaucracy rules

The bridge will become politically fragile if it feels like another maze. It should therefore rely on automatic administration wherever possible.

Recommended position:

- bank accrual should be driven automatically from payroll-tax records rather than household-by-household manual applications
- bridge activation should be triggered automatically or near-automatically when verified coverage loss, hours loss, or similar transition events are detected
- households should not have to re-prove information the state or administrator already has
- bridge renewal within an active bank month should be automatic absent a clear disqualifying event
- notices should be short, plain-language, and state the hierarchy clearly: what is covered by basic care, what remains under Medi-Cal or private coverage, and when the bridge is active

The bridge should be legible enough that ordinary households can use it without hiring experts to navigate it.

#### Earned Healthcare Continuity Insurance and Medi-Cal

The framework should say plainly that earned healthcare continuity insurance is not the same thing as Medi-Cal, even though it overlaps with services Medi-Cal now covers.

Medi-Cal is a broader public coverage program. It already covers emergency care, hospitalization, specialty care, broader prescription-drug coverage, and other medically necessary services for eligible members. The framework should build around that existing federal-state platform rather than implying that California should first rewrite Medi-Cal eligibility and replace the federal match with a state-only substitute.

The framework should hold the following line:

> Basic care is the universal baseline. Medi-Cal remains the broader public coverage system for eligible Californians. Earned healthcare continuity insurance is the work-earned transition major-medical bridge for non-basic costs when ordinary coverage fails and adequate Medi-Cal or private coverage does not exist. The point is to reduce cliffs and coverage churn now, while leaving room for broader federal repair later.

For services that fall inside the defined `basic care` package, payment should flow through the basic-care financing system rather than through Medi-Cal. For services outside the basic-care package, the same order applies: Medi-Cal first where it already governs, adequate private coverage next where it exists, and earned healthcare continuity insurance only as the work-earned continuity layer.

The key point is that basic care is universal, Medi-Cal remains the backbone public program, and earned healthcare continuity insurance governs broader protection beyond that floor only where the existing major systems do not adequately cover the household during coverage disruption.

## Implementation Questions

### Administration and Delivery

The work-earned continuity major-medical layer should be easy to understand at the point of care. It should not compete by narrowing networks.

#### Network and administration discipline

If a qualified provider or facility delivers a covered earned-healthcare-continuity-insurance service, the continuity layer should have a way to pay that claim under statewide rules. Competition among administrators should happen on claims execution, fraud control, data quality, and operational competence, not on excluding doctors, clinics, hospitals, or specialists from the continuity layer.

Recommended position:

- earned healthcare continuity insurance should use the same regional administrative logic already proposed for the basic-care layer
- provider qualification should follow existing licensure, credentialing, and billing standards
- a central provider registry should identify qualified providers, facilities, and regional administrator assignment
- the registry should be privately administered under state contract and publicly available
- administrators should not narrow continuity insurance through selective provider exclusion

This keeps continuity insurance legible. The system should tell a patient whether a service is covered, not force the patient to guess whether a covered service happened to be rendered by the wrong contracting entity.

#### Fraud control and appeals

Because continuity insurance should be broadly usable, it also needs visible integrity and dispute rules.

Recommended position:

- use prepayment edits, post-payment audit authority, and outlier review for obvious abuse
- maintain a clear appeal path for denied or downcoded claims
- publish administrator performance on denials, reversals, fraud findings, and payment timeliness
- reward real fraud prevention and recovery, not blanket denial behavior

Continuity insurance should be easy to use for legitimate care and hard to exploit at scale.

### Cost Sharing

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should have **disciplined cost sharing** that moderates use of non-basic care while still protecting households from catastrophe.

#### Core principle

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should have enough cost sharing to preserve incentive and discipline, but never enough to expose a household to catastrophic financial collapse.

#### Recommended position

- basic care remains free at point of use
- earned healthcare continuity insurance has higher copays and other cost sharing than strong private insurance
- earned healthcare continuity insurance always includes a hard annual household out-of-pocket ceiling
- the out-of-pocket ceiling should be low enough to prevent medical bankruptcy and destabilizing debt

This is the correct distinction:

- earned healthcare continuity insurance = safe but disciplined
- private coverage = richer, lower-friction, and lower cost sharing

#### Practical structure

Without fixing exact numbers yet, the framework should assume:

- meaningful specialist copays
- meaningful ER and hospital cost sharing within program rules
- meaningful imaging and surgery cost sharing
- stronger patient exposure than strong private insurance
- a hard annual household maximum beyond which continuity insurance fully protects against further catastrophic cost

This means continuity insurance should feel clearly less generous than strong private coverage, but still humane enough that a serious illness does not turn into family-level financial ruin.

### Financing

The non-basic bridge layer should be financed separately from the universal basic-care fund.

Recommended position:

- the basic-care fund pays covered universal basic-care claims
- earned healthcare continuity insurance is financed through a household-month hours-bank structure supported by payroll-tax contributions and related financing rules
- federal Medicaid financing should be preserved wherever Medi-Cal eligibility and federal match already exist
- the two layers should remain distinct so the universal baseline is not destabilized by the higher cost of emergency, hospital, specialty, and other non-basic care

In the California bridge phase, the financing standard should stay modest and durable. The continuity layer should be sized first around layoffs, cliff churn, hours loss, and ordinary transition shocks.

Recommended fiscal guardrails:

- use a modest payroll-contribution launch rate sized around the bridge function rather than a full replacement model
- treat roughly `0.10%` of payroll as a floor for launch sizing, with a more durable planning band around `0.12%` to `0.15%`
- allow automatic rate adjustment inside a defined solvency band, for example up to roughly `0.20%`, if claims or reserve levels require it
- reserve temporary recession authority above the normal band, for example up to roughly `0.25%`, only under clearly defined stress conditions
- maintain a visible reserve floor so the bridge does not become unstable in downturns; a reasonable starting principle is a reserve equal to at least `3` months of expected bridge claims
- require public solvency reporting so contribution changes, reserve drawdowns, and bridge costs remain visible rather than hidden

These guardrails matter politically as much as fiscally. They show that the bridge is finite, financed, and rule-governed rather than open-ended.

If federal reforms later make it practical to integrate more of the patchwork into this same model, that should be treated as a later stage with separate federal legislation and financing, not as a hidden assumption of the California bridge phase.

### Tests and Metrics

The earned healthcare continuity insurance model needs tests and metrics strong enough to guide design choices and public evaluation.

#### 1. Work-Incentive Test

Is the system removing healthcare cliffs rather than recreating them?

Possible metrics:

- share of workers who maintain healthcare protection after increasing hours or wages
- average earned months of household bridge coverage per month of qualifying work
- bank depletion rate after qualifying transitions
- average bank balance in household-month units by wage band
- churn rate into and out of bridge coverage
- continuity rates through voluntary work transitions and entrepreneurship

#### 2. Emergency-Use Discipline Test

Is the system preserving emergency access while reducing misuse?

Possible metrics:

- non-emergent ER visit rate
- share of ER visits reimbursed at urgent-care-equivalent logic
- repeat non-emergent ER use rate
- percentage of births fully covered without separate eligibility friction

#### 3. Adequacy and Handoff Test

Is the handoff between earned healthcare continuity insurance, private coverage, and the marketplace functioning clearly?

Possible metrics:

- share of households moved off continuity insurance because of genuinely adequate private coverage
- rate of appeals or disputes over adequacy determinations
- continuity of coverage during voluntary, involuntary, and cliff-driven transitions
- share of banked households preserving credits while under adequate private coverage
- difference in average patient cost sharing between continuity insurance and strong private coverage
- share of continuity-insurance households hitting the annual out-of-pocket cap
- rate of automatic versus manual bridge activation
- rate of child cases protected through the administrative-gap safeguard

#### 4. Fiscal Durability Test

Is the earned healthcare continuity insurance layer stable enough to last?

Possible metrics:

- solvency and draw rate of the continuity-insurance hours bank
- annual payroll-tax revenue into the continuity-insurance pool
- average household-month balance by region and wage band
- reserve adequacy against the defined bridge solvency floor
- frequency and magnitude of automatic payroll-rate adjustments

## Conclusion

Earned healthcare continuity insurance should be understood as work-earned transition major-medical protection, not as a vague promise of comprehensive public insurance.

Its purpose is narrower and more durable: keep serious illness, emergency care, hospitalization, and specialty treatment from destroying a household's financial stability during coverage disruption, while preserving work incentives and keeping the path into stronger private coverage open.

If the framework stays clear on that identity, the rest of the design becomes easier to defend. Basic care remains the universal baseline. Earned healthcare continuity insurance remains the work-earned transition major-medical bridge. Private coverage remains the richer tier above it. The bridge is earned, finite, and rule-governed. It is not an unlimited replacement for the rest of the system.

The point is simple: serious illness should not destroy a working household.

### Open Questions

This document still leaves several questions open for later refinement:

- how qualifying work should be defined for purposes of earning household-month bank credits
- how exactly payroll-tax financing for the bridge layer should be split, collected, and adjusted inside the solvency band
- where to set the income threshold for draw suspension from the bridge bank
- what limited Medi-Cal alignment changes, waivers, or state-plan adjustments might improve handoffs without making the framework depend on a wholesale eligibility rewrite
- how private insurance should interact with the household-month continuity-insurance layer
- what exact adequacy standard should determine when private coverage displaces continuity insurance
- where exactly to set copays, coinsurance, and the annual household out-of-pocket ceiling
- what later federal reforms would be required if the bridge model were eventually broadened beyond the California-first continuity phase
